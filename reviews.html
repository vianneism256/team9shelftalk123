Janel Laynes rated 4.8

Where the Crawdads Sing by Delia Owens

Just finished *Where the Crawdads Sing* by Delia Owens, and I'm speechless. The storytelling is beautiful, and the twist at the end really took me by surprise. Kya's journey of survival and self-discovery is so moving. I couldn't put the book down once I started, and I was on the edge of my seat with every page. The descriptions of the natural world are vivid and poetic, making you feel like you're right there with Kya in the swamps. Highly recommend this one to anyone who enjoys a heartfelt, gripping story.

Gabriel Ibo rated 4.5

The Fault in Our Stars by John Green

Reading *The Fault in Our Stars* by John Green was an emotional rollercoaster. It’s not often that a book makes you laugh, cry, and think all at once. Hazel and Gus are such memorable characters—beautifully written and relatable. The way Green handles topics like illness and love is both delicate and powerful. I found myself highlighting many passages throughout the book, as they spoke to me on a deep level. It's a story that will stay with you long after you’ve finished it. A must-read for anyone looking for a meaningful, thought-provoking novel.

Kiara Lohan Ella rated 5.0

The Institute by Stephen King

*The Institute* by Stephen King is a chilling and intense read. It kept me up at night, turning pages as fast as I could. The characters were well-developed, especially Luke, who is one of the most relatable and resilient protagonists I've read in a while. King’s exploration of power dynamics, both in the government and in the minds of children with extraordinary abilities, is a great commentary on society. The tension in the book builds slowly but steadily, and the stakes are so high that you can’t help but keep reading. If you're a fan of suspense and thrillers, this one is a must-read!

Vianne Louie Navida rated 4.7

The Night Circus by Erin Morgenstern

*The Night Circus* by Erin Morgenstern is a beautifully imaginative story that will transport you into a world of magic and wonder. The narrative weaves between different timelines and characters, slowly building the mysterious and magical atmosphere of the circus. I loved how Morgenstern explores the tension between the two magicians, and the concept of a rivalry between two sides with such high stakes was incredibly compelling. The descriptions of the circus and its wonders were so vivid that I could practically see the scenes unfolding in front of me. It’s a book that takes its time to immerse you in its world, and once you’re there, you won’t want to leave.

Ariana Blake rated 4.3

Looking for Alaska by John Green

I've just finished *Looking for Alaska* by John Green, and I can say without a doubt that it's one of the most powerful and poignant books I've ever read. The story about Miles “Pudge” Halter’s journey to find a deeper meaning in life at boarding school is a coming-of-age narrative that hits all the right notes. The characters are flawed, realistic, and incredibly relatable. Alaska Young is a fascinating character, and her story is both heartbreaking and mysterious. I especially appreciated the way the book explores grief, loss, and the complexities of human relationships. If you haven't read it yet, I highly recommend it.

Ryan Jameson rated 4.6

Normal People by Sally Rooney

*Normal People* by Sally Rooney is a beautifully written exploration of love, friendship, and the complexities of growing up. The chemistry between Connell and Marianne is palpable, and their journey through high school and university is both raw and incredibly moving. The way Rooney captures the subtleties of relationships, the awkwardness, and the small but significant moments is nothing short of brilliant. It’s one of those books that make you reflect on your own relationships and the way we grow and change over time. I highly recommend it to anyone who loves character-driven stories.

Chloe Adams rated 4.2

The Great Gatsby by F. Scott Fitzgerald

*The Great Gatsby* by F. Scott Fitzgerald is a classic that deserves all the praise it gets. The story of Jay Gatsby’s unrelenting pursuit of Daisy Buchanan is a poignant portrayal of the American Dream and its ultimate emptiness. Fitzgerald’s writing is elegant, and the themes of love, wealth, and social class are timeless. However, while the story is fascinating, I did find some of the characters a little hard to connect with. Still, it’s an important read, and the novel's exploration of ambition, illusion, and the pursuit of happiness is worth diving into.

Noah Bennett rated 4.9

Circe by Madeline Miller

*Circe* by Madeline Miller is a brilliant retelling of Greek mythology from the perspective of Circe, the powerful witch from Homer's *Odyssey*. Miller’s writing is lyrical and captivating, and the way she brings Circe's character to life is nothing short of extraordinary. I loved how the book explores themes of power, love, and loneliness, and how Circe navigates her complex relationships with the gods and mortals. It’s a fresh and modern take on an ancient myth, and it will resonate with anyone who has ever felt like an outsider. Definitely one of my favorite books this year!

Emily Torres rated 4.4

Becoming by Michelle Obama

Michelle Obama’s memoir, *Becoming*, is an inspiring and moving read. The way she opens up about her personal experiences, struggles, and triumphs is incredibly powerful. From her childhood in Chicago to her time in the White House, Obama’s story is one of resilience, determination, and grace. I appreciated how authentic and honest she is throughout the book, sharing both her successes and her vulnerabilities. It’s not just a memoir about a famous First Lady—it’s a story about family, identity, and finding your own voice. Highly recommend to anyone looking for inspiration and a deeper understanding of the woman behind the public persona.

Lucas Palmer rated 4.5

Educated by Tara Westover

*Educated* by Tara Westover is a raw and powerful memoir about a young girl who grows up in a strict, survivalist family in rural Idaho. Westover’s story of self-education and escaping her family’s grasp is both heartbreaking and inspiring. Her journey from ignorance to knowledge is not just an academic one, but a deeply personal transformation. The book is a poignant reminder of the power of education to change lives, and it will make you reflect on the importance of freedom and self-determination. It’s one of the most moving memoirs I’ve read in a long time.
